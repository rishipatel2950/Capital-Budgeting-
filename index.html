Beta (β) = SLOPE(Hemingway, S&P 500)β = 1.1770 — Hemingway is 17.7% more volatile than the market
Interpreting Beta = 1.177: For every 1% the market moves, Hemingway is expected to move 1.177% in the same direction. Years 3 and 7 confirm this — Hemingway fell harder than the market in downturns. This higher volatility reflects the discretionary nature of premium fishing equipment: consumers cut spending on luxury goods first during recessions, amplifying Hemingway's cyclical risk. Beta > 1 means investors demand a higher return to hold this stock, which is why the cost of equity (10.47%) exceeds a lower-beta company.

§ 04Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)
WACC Formula
WACC = wd × rd(1−T) + we × re

Where rd = blended cost of debt:
= (wbond × YTM) + (wLOC × rLOC)
= (0.75 × 6.59%) + (0.25 × 7.74%)
= 4.94% + 1.94% = 6.88% (before tax)

After-tax cost of debt:
= 6.88% × (1 − 21%) = 5.43%

WACC = 0.30 × 5.43% + 0.70 × 10.47%
= 1.63% + 7.33% = 8.96%

§ 06Final Recommendation & Strategic Rationale
ACCEPT PROJECT B
Mechanized Large-Scale Production — Hemingway Tarpon Classic
Based on the capital budgeting analysis, Project B should be accepted. Using a WACC of 8.96% as the discount rate, Project B generates an NPV of $145,767 and an IRR of 9.59% — both superior to Project A's NPV of $82,899 and IRR of 9.27%. Additionally, Project B's consistent and immediate cash inflows reduce payback risk and improve liquidity, while Project A's delayed back-loaded structure creates greater exposure to uncertainty during the critical early ramp-up period.
Why Project B Wins — 4 Key Reasons
1
Higher NPV — Project B Creates $62,868 More Shareholder Value
Project B's NPV of $145,767 exceeds Project A's $82,899 by $62,868 — a 75.8% advantage. NPV is the gold standard capital budgeting metric because it directly measures the net present-value dollars added to the firm after accounting for the time value of money. Since both projects require the exact same $5.25M upfront investment, Project B is the clear winner: it creates significantly more wealth for Hemingway shareholders per dollar of capital deployed.
2
Higher IRR — Greater Safety Margin Above the WACC Hurdle Rate
Project B's IRR of 9.59% clears the WACC hurdle of 8.96% by 63 basis points, versus Project A's IRR of 9.27% which only clears the hurdle by 31 bps. A wider IRR spread means Project B is more resilient to estimation error — if the WACC were revised upward to 9.5% (well within the range of estimation uncertainty), Project A would become NPV-negative while Project B remains solidly profitable. Project B provides more breathing room for real-world deviation from assumptions.
3
Immediate and Consistent Cash Flows Reduce Payback Risk
Project B generates $800K in Year 1 versus Project A's $100K — 8x more in the most critical first year. Project B also maintains highly stable inflows of $860K across Years 3 through 8 (a flat, predictable stream), while Project A is highly uneven — peaking at $1,600K in Year 5 before declining sharply. Early cash recovery reduces Hemingway's exposure to future uncertainty, improves working capital, reduces reinvestment risk, and allows the company to fund its next product generation sooner. By the time Project A peaks, Project B has already recouped most of its investment.
4
Scalability and Market Reach — Serves Both Professional and Recreational Anglers
Project B's mechanized large-scale production enables Hemingway to build inventory quickly and price the Tarpon Classic competitively for both tournament professionals and the growing recreational angler market. Project A's custom hand-tooling is inherently capacity-constrained — proficiency takes time to develop, limiting unit output. Project B captures the volume opportunity in the fastest-growing market segment, directly aligned with Hemingway's strategy of leveraging the tournament fishing boom into broader brand recognition and mass-market revenue growth.
Project A — Key Limitations
✗
Back-Loaded Cash Flows — Only $100K in Year 1 vs. $800K for Project B
Project A generates only $100K in Year 1 and $500K in Year 2. These minimal early inflows reflect the time required for machinists to develop hand-tooling proficiency. The company has already spent $5.25M upfront — meaning Hemingway carries full capital risk with minimal early return. The peak cash flows in Years 5–6 ($1,600K and $1,500K) come only after a long delay, and by that time the time-value discount erodes much of their NPV contribution.
✗
Operational Risk — Dependent on Artisanal Machinist Skill Development
Project A's revenue model depends entirely on machinists developing hand-tooling proficiency over time — a process the case compares to watch-making. If training is slower than anticipated, turnover is high, or quality falls short of customer expectations, cash flows in Years 1–3 could be materially worse. This human-capital dependency is a significant execution risk that Project B's mechanized process does not carry.
✗
Lower NPV and IRR on Every Capital Budgeting Metric
Project A's NPV of $82,899 is 43% lower than Project B's $145,767. Its IRR of 9.27% clears the WACC hurdle by only 31 basis points. If WACC were revised upward to 9.5% — well within estimation error — Project A becomes NPV-negative while Project B remains solidly profitable at every metric.
✗
Limited Market Reach — Premium Pricing Narrows the Customer Base
Custom hand-tooled products command premium prices that appeal only to a small segment of serious tournament anglers. Hemingway's stated strategy includes capturing recreational angler demand — a much larger market that Project A's high-cost, low-volume approach cannot serve effectively.
✗
Declining Cash Flows — Market Saturation and Durability Risk
Project A's cash flows fall sharply from $1,600K (Year 5) to $600K (Year 10) — a 63% drop — as the custom market saturates. High-quality hand-tooled reels rarely fail or wear out, reducing repeat purchase cycles. This saturation dynamic reinforces the concentrated risk of the hand-tooling model in a niche market with a small, slow-growing customer base.
